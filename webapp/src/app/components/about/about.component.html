Our History

George and Will Laing walked from Saskatoon, SK in the fall of 1903 to choose a homestead. The two brothers got to the top of the big hill on Sweetgrass Reserve and could see west for miles. There were rows of corner mounds marking the quarter sections. George took a homestead, NE 27-43-20 W3, in 1902 in the Gallivan area. The area thrived with new young families and Georgesâ€™s son Bill Laing was born on November 5, 1925. He purchased the home quarter SW 26-43-20 W3 in 1949 from George Blackstock. He farmed with his brothers Gordon and Calvin as a mixed operation until 1957. On the livestock side, they raised cattle, pigs, chickens, and milking cow. On the grain side of their operation they grew wheat, barley and oats.

Bill farmed on his own from 1957 until his son, Les, joined formally in 1974. Les had obtained his Diploma in Agriculture in 1975 and they farmed together until 2000, when Bill passed away. Willow View has had the help of foreign exchange youth since 1992. It is with the help of international employees that the farm has continued to grow. Les continues to buy and rent additional land. The farm was incorporated March 31, 2008 under Willow View Farm Ltd. The cattle operation was discontinued in 2009. Crops grown are wheat, canola, field peas and barley.

Land Base

Willow View operations are based in northwest Saskatchewan. The business consists of a combination of owned and rented land as well as customized contract farming agreements. Currently the land base is used to grow canola, field peas, wheat and barley.

In Saskatchewan, the soils were developed in glacial deposits which has resulted in a variety of soil zones that exist in the province. Willow View farms in the Black soil zone, which has a darker colour and contains high levels of organic matter of the surface soil horizons.

The mapping of soil zones tends to reflect the climatic gradient.The Black soil zone is in a transitional environment between forest and grassland. In north Saskatchewan, the climate is cooler and humid. In south Saskatchewan, the climate is dry and warm. Temperatures vary greatly between seasons. In the crop year, there are highs of 30 degrees Celsius and lows of 8 degrees Celsius

A single crop is grown each year over the course of three to four months. Seeding commences in May, followed by in-crop spraying in June, fungicide application in July, desiccation in August and harvest in September.
